Sustainable Practices

Exploring best practices in water management, pesticide use, and energy efficiency for a more sustainable cannabis industry.

Water Management

Water management is a critical area in sustainable cannabis cultivation. Efficient extraction of water, both in timing and quantity, can significantly reduce the environmental impact of cannabis production. For instance, implementing systems like rainwater storage and automated irrigation, specifically drip irrigation and moisture sensors, can help minimize water waste. These methods, combined with water treatment and reuse strategies, are essential in conserving water resources, particularly in drought-prone regions where large-scale cannabis cultivation can exacerbate water scarcity (Wartenberg, 2021).

Pesticide Use

Pesticide use in cannabis cultivation requires careful consideration to ensure sustainability. Developing rigorous testing standards for contaminants in cannabis products is vital to protect both consumers and the environment. Certification schemes and educational programs can help producers adopt alternative pest control methods, reducing the reliance on harmful chemicals. By incentivizing the use of biologically derived pesticide products and implementing integrated pest management methods, the industry can mitigate the risks associated with pesticide misuse (MJH Life Sciences, 2023). Additionally, beneficial insects can be introduced to the production process to reduce the need for synthetic pesticides and promote plant growth.

Energy Efficiency

Energy efficiency is another crucial aspect of sustainable cannabis production. Cultivation, particularly indoor growing, often requires substantial energy inputs for lighting, heating, cooling, and ventilation. To manage this, regulations should require growers to submit energy plans that comply with existing standards and report on their energy usage. Prioritizing the use of energy-efficient light sources, renewable energy, and scheduling operations around peak demand times can significantly reduce the energy footprint of cannabis cultivation (Erickson, 2023). Particularly, energy-efficient LED lights for cannabis cultivation can use less electricity, produce less heat, and allow better control of light. This combined with systems to monitor and optimize energy use, using timers, sensors, and automated controls to regulate lights, HVAC, and other equipment, can significantly save energy.

Air Pollution Control

Air pollution control measures are also important in reducing the environmental impact of cannabis cultivation. Permits for cultivation facilities should be issued based on ozone-formation simulations to minimize air quality issues. Additionally, there should be greater transparency regarding the use of BVOCs (Biogenic Volatile Organic Compounds) and masking agents in cultivation facilities, particularly in terms of their effects on human health. Implementing carbon filtration and closed-loop extraction systems can further help mitigate air pollution associated with cannabis production (MJH Life Sciences, 2023).

Compost and Organic Fertilizers

Enhancing soil fertility while avoiding harmful chemicals is achievable through the use of compost of plant trimmings and organic fertilizers. These organic methods not only reduce the need for synthetic fertilizers but also improve nutrient availability and plant growth, promoting a healthier and more sustainable growing environment, closing the loop in waste management.

Sustainable Packaging

The choice of product packaging plays a significant role in sustainability. Opting for eco-friendly, compostable packaging made from recycled or biodegradable materials can greatly reduce the environmental impact. Additionally, minimizing unnecessary packaging is key to lowering waste.

Proper Disposal of Waste

Proper disposal of hazardous waste is crucial to avoid environmental contamination. Following local regulations for disposing of used nutrient solutions, chemical containers, and other hazardous materials ensures that these substances do not harm the ecosystem. Working with specialized waste management companies can help in managing cannabis-related waste responsibly.

Protecting the Environment

Preserving and protecting natural habitats around cannabis cultivation areas is vital for maintaining local ecosystems. Efforts such as keeping native plants, creating wildlife corridors, and minimizing disruption to the environment can help safeguard biodiversity. Additionally, adopting pollinator-friendly practices, like planting native flowering plants and creating habitats for bees and butterflies, supports the health of the ecosystem.

Producing Hemp

Marijuana and hemp belong to the same plant species (i.e., Cannabis sativa), but hemp is genetically different and also distinguished by its use and chemical makeup, (0.3% THC or less). Research has found that hemp has been re-discovered as a sustainable, high yielding industrial fiber crop that can help meet the high global demand for fibers. Additionally, the USDA can actually fund research on hemp, defined as cannabis with no more than 0.3% ∆9-tetrahydrocannabinol (THC) on a dry-weight basis, thanks to legislation that Congress passed in 2018 to legalize hemp in the US. But the USDA still lacks the authority to fund research on cannabis that does not meet the hemp definition.

Here are some ways that hemp is sustainable:

  • Hemp fiber can be used for textiles, clothing, as biocomposites for automobiles, paper, building materials (hempcrete), animal bedding, and has potential as a bio-fuel crop too
  • Bast fibers can account for 20-30% of the stalks and are used in the automotive and the paper industry. Hurd fibers, on the other hand, make up 70-80% of the stalk and typically contain 20-30% lignin, which is used for used as a livestock bedding, and other construction applications including fiberboard
  • Hemp is one of the most efficient plants for converting atmospheric CO2 into biomass. It can absorb more CO2 per hectare than many other crops or even forests, thus playing a significant role in mitigating climate change.
  • Hemp biomass can be used to produce bioenergy, including biochar, biogas, and ethanol. These renewable energy sources help reduce reliance on fossil fuels, further supporting environmental sustainability.
  • Hemp generally requires fewer pesticides and herbicides compared to other crops due to its natural resistance to pests and its ability to suppress weeds. This reduces the chemical load on the environment, contributing to more sustainable agricultural practices.
  • Promotes resource efficiency. The plant's entire biomass can be utilized, minimizing waste.

Certification Programs

Participating in certification programs can help increase sustainability, here are some of the programs and what participating in them entails:

  • Sun+Earth Certified
    • Guarantees that Sun+Earth Certified cannabis is grown using regenerative organic farming practices without the application of pesticides and other toxic chemicals.
    • Ensures that workers have reasonable workplace conditions and the right to a living wage and community engagement, which ensures that farmers engage with and give back to their respective communities.
  • Clean Green Certified
    • Growing procedures are reviewed and confirmed that they meet the program requirements.
    • Source of water and electricity, soil erosion protection, border areas, pest/weed/disease control, are questioned and sometimes inspected.
    • Plants are examined for excess molds, mildews, pest problems, or other agricultural problems.
