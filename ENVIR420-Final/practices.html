Sustainable Practices

Exploring best practices in water management, pesticide use, and energy efficiency for a more sustainable cannabis industry.

Water Management

Water management practices include:

  • Efficient water extraction timing and amounts
  • Incentivizing efficient water usage
  • Methods like rainwater storage, automated irrigation, and water treatment and reuse to reduce waste
  • Implementing moisture sensors or a drip irrigation to save water

Pesticide Use

Sustainable practices for pesticide use include:

  • Rigorous testing standards for contaminants
  • Certification schemes and education for producers on alternative pest control methods
  • Incentives to avoid the use of illegal chemicals
  • Use beneficial insects, organic pesticides, or other natural controls

Energy Efficiency

Energy efficiency can be improved by:

  • Submitting energy plans that comply with standards
  • Using energy-efficient light sources, such as LED lights, which produce less heat and allow better control of light
  • Opt towards using renewable energy sources, like solar panels or wind turbines
  • Scheduling operations around peak demand times
  • Setting up systems using timers, sensors, and automated controls to regulate lights, HVAC, and other equipment.

Air Pollution Control

Air pollution control measures include:

  • Using ozone-formation simulations for permit issuance
  • Carbon filtration and closed-loop extraction systems

Compost and Organic Fertilizers

Looking into composting and organic fertilizers could:

  • Reduce the need for synthetic fertilizers
  • Enhance soil fertility, nutrient availability, and plant growth.
  • Reduce landfill waste

Sustainable Packaging

Using alternative packaging, like eco-friendly/compostable packaging made from recycled or biodegradable materials could:

  • Minimize unnecessary packaging to lower environmental impact
  • Reduce dependence on materials like plastic, reducing unnecessary landfill waste

Protecting the Environment

Ways to protect the environment while producing cannabis include:

  • Encouraging pollinators like bees and butterflies by planting or keeping native flowering plants and creating habitats for them
  • Creating wildlife corridors
  • Minimizing disruption to local ecosystems

Producing Hemp

Marijuana and hemp belong to the same plant species (i.e., Cannabis sativa), but hemp is genetically different and also distinguished by its use and chemical makeup, (0.3% THC or less). Research has found that hemp has been re-discovered as a sustainable, high yielding industrial fiber crop that can help meet the high global demand for fibers. Additionally, the USDA can actually fund research on hemp, defined as cannabis with no more than 0.3% ∆9-tetrahydrocannabinol (THC) on a dry-weight basis, thanks to legislation that Congress passed in 2018 to legalize hemp in the US. But the USDA still lacks the authority to fund research on cannabis that does not meet the hemp definition.

Here are some ways that hemp is sustainable:

  • Hemp fiber can be used for textiles, clothing, as biocomposites for automobiles, paper, building materials (hempcrete), animal bedding, and has potential as a bio-fuel crop too
  • Bast fibers can account for 20-30% of the stalks and are used in the automotive and the paper industry. Hurd fibers, on the other hand, make up 70-80% of the stalk and typically contain 20-30% lignin, which is used for used as a livestock bedding, and other construction applications including fiberboard
  • Hemp is one of the most efficient plants for converting atmospheric CO2 into biomass. It can absorb more CO2 per hectare than many other crops or even forests, thus playing a significant role in mitigating climate change.
  • Hemp biomass can be used to produce bioenergy, including biochar, biogas, and ethanol. These renewable energy sources help reduce reliance on fossil fuels, further supporting environmental sustainability.
  • Hemp generally requires fewer pesticides and herbicides compared to other crops due to its natural resistance to pests and its ability to suppress weeds. This reduces the chemical load on the environment, contributing to more sustainable agricultural practices.
  • Promotes resource efficiency. The plant's entire biomass can be utilized, minimizing waste.

Certification Programs

Participating in certification programs can help increase sustainability, here are some of the programs and what participating in them entails:

  • Sun+Earth Certified
    • Guarantees that Sun+Earth Certified cannabis is grown using regenerative organic farming practices without the application of pesticides and other toxic chemicals.
    • Ensures that workers have reasonable workplace conditions and the right to a living wage and community engagement, which ensures that farmers engage with and give back to their respective communities.
  • Clean Green Certified
    • Growing procedures are reviewed and confirmed that they meet the program requirements.
    • Source of water and electricity, soil erosion protection, border areas, pest/weed/disease control, are questioned and sometimes inspected.
    • Plants are examined for excess molds, mildews, pest problems, or other agricultural problems.
