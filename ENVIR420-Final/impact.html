Environmental Impact

Exploring the environmental concerns and impact of cannabis production, focusing on energy use, water consumption, and pesticide management.

Cannabis Supply Chain

The cannabis supply chain is complex and involves multiple stages, each with its own set of challenges and environmental impacts. The five main stages—Cultivation, Manufacturing, Distribution, Transportation, and Retail—are all interconnected, with each stage depending on the successful completion of the previous one. For example, cultivation must produce high-quality cannabis that is then processed and packaged during the manufacturing stage before being distributed to retail outlets. Throughout this supply chain, Track and Trace systems are implemented to ensure that every product is compliant with state regulations, helping to maintain transparency and accountability within the industry (Distru, 2020).

The cultivation of cannabis is particularly resource-intensive, involving six distinct stages: Germination, Seeding, Vegetative, Pre-flowering, Flowering, and Harvesting. After these stages, post-harvest processes such as drying, curing, and trimming prepare the cannabis for the market. These stages require careful management of resources, including water and energy, to minimize environmental impact while maximizing yield (Distru, 2020).

Manufacturing

The manufacturing stage involves several critical processes that transform raw cannabis into marketable products. These processes include extraction—using either solvent or non-solvent methods—light manufacturing for products like pre-rolls, and infusion for edible products (Distru, 2020). Each of these processes must adhere to strict regulatory standards to ensure product safety and quality. Additionally, the choice of manufacturing methods can significantly influence the environmental footprint of cannabis production, with some methods being more sustainable than others.

Distribution

Distribution in the cannabis industry is complicated by a variety of challenges, particularly those related to state-specific regulations. These regulations govern everything from the size of vehicles that can be used for transportation to the prohibition of interstate transport, which restricts the movement of cannabis products across state lines (Distru, 2020). Intra-state transport is also heavily regulated, requiring businesses to navigate a complex legal landscape to ensure compliance and avoid penalties.

Transportation

Transportation is another critical component of the cannabis supply chain, requiring specific licenses and adherence to strict regulations. Testing plays a vital role at this stage to ensure that the cannabis being transported is safe and free from contaminants. This includes rigorous checks for contamination, pesticide residues, potency levels, and terpene profiles (Distru, 2020). These tests not only ensure consumer safety but also help maintain the integrity of the legal market by preventing substandard products from reaching the shelves.

Retail

The retail stage presents its own set of challenges, particularly in ensuring that products are presented and sold in compliance with state regulations. Retailers must be knowledgeable about their products and able to educate consumers, especially given the complex regulatory environment. They also face the challenge of competing with the black market, which often offers lower prices but without the same safety and quality assurances. This competition makes it essential for legal retailers to emphasize the benefits of purchasing regulated products (Distru, 2020).

Environmental Concerns in Cannabis Production

The environmental impact of cannabis production is significant, particularly when it comes to the use of energy, water, and pesticides. Indoor cultivation, which is common in many regions, requires substantial energy inputs for lighting, heating, cooling, dehumidifying, and ventilation. This high energy use contributes to the overall carbon footprint of the cannabis industry (Wartenberg, 2021). Additionally, water usage is a critical concern, especially in drier climates where water is already a scarce resource. Efficient water management practices are essential to reducing the environmental impact of cannabis cultivation.

Pesticide use in cannabis production is another area of concern, as the misuse or overuse of pesticides can pose significant risks to workers, consumers, and the environment. Pesticides can contaminate water sources, harm non-target species, and contribute to the development of pesticide-resistant pests (Wartenberg, 2021). Addressing these issues requires rigorous testing and the adoption of safer, more sustainable pest management practices.

Why do these concerns exist?

There are multiple reasons why these concerns exist and they mainly revolve around legalization issues. Some of them include:

  • The federal prohibition means that the Department of Energy and other federal agencies can't fund research to determine how to improve energy efficiency and minimize carbon dioxide emissions at cannabis cultivation facilities.
  • The Environmental Protection Agency is barred from researching cannabis production to determine air-emission factors, which companies use to estimate how much air pollution they produce.
  • Cannabis growers can't get federal tax breaks for installing new technologies to reduce their energy use, as other industries can.
  • There aren't decades of agricultural research on how to optimize cannabis growing conditions in greenhouses and indoor facilities.
  • Most states where cannabis is legal, including Colorado, do not require cultivation facilities to meet specific energy standards, though Illinois and Massachusetts do.

Policy Recommendations

Land Use

Effective land use planning is crucial for minimizing the negative environmental impacts of cannabis cultivation. Strategies could include regulating the location and size of new farming areas, relocating existing farms to more suitable land, and implementing wildlife-conscious farming practices (Wartenberg, 2021). These measures would help protect vulnerable ecosystems and prevent the negative consequences of poorly planned cultivation sites.

Water Use

Water management is another critical area where policy interventions could have a significant impact. Establishing water licensing systems and incentivizing efficient water usage are two strategies that could help alleviate the pressure on water resources, particularly in semi-arid and drought-prone areas. Additionally, promoting the use of rainwater storage, automated irrigation, and water treatment and reuse systems could further reduce water waste in cannabis cultivation (Wartenberg, 2021).

Pesticide Use

To address the challenges associated with pesticide use, it is essential to develop rigorous testing standards for contaminants in cannabis products. Certification schemes and educational programs for producers can encourage the adoption of alternative pest control methods, reducing the reliance on harmful chemicals (MJH Life Sciences, 2023). Furthermore, thorough supply chain tracing and environmental monitoring are necessary to prevent the illegal sourcing of cannabis and to promote safer, more sustainable production practices.

Energy Use

Managing energy use in the cannabis industry is critical to reducing its overall environmental footprint. Policies should require growers to submit energy plans that comply with existing standards and report on their energy usage regularly. Encouraging the use of energy-efficient lighting, renewable energy sources, and optimized scheduling of operations around peak demand times are all best practices that could contribute to a more sustainable cannabis industry (Erickson, 2023).

Air Pollution

Air pollution from cannabis cultivation and processing facilities is another area where policy could play a vital role. Permits should be issued based on ozone-formation simulations, and there should be greater transparency regarding the use of BVOCs (Biogenic Volatile Organic Compounds) and masking agents that could affect human health. Implementing carbon filtration and closed-loop extraction systems are additional measures that could help mitigate the air quality impacts of cannabis production (MJH Life Sciences, 2023).
